title = "Learning the Subject or Learning the Exam?"
tags = ["education", "learning"]
+++

As a student, I have often wondered whether _getting good_ in education necessarily means learning. The question arose not from reading educational research, but from observing of my friends--asking me on _how to prepare for examinations?_.

> TLDR:
> A realization that has stayed with me for years began with something surprisingly ordinary: studying with friends before an exam.
I used to feel _*_ whenever a friend asked me to study together. I would happily agree, thinking we would spend time understanding the subject from the ground up. When discussing mathematics or physics, I preferred to start from the fundamental concepts and gradually build toward the formulas and procedures. 
Yet the conversation would often go differently.
After a few minutes, they would interrupt and say something along the lines of, "Can we skip this part? Which section is likely to appear on the exam?" or "Just show me the steps needed to solve the question."
_I found this attitude disappointing_. The goal is clear: maximize marks while minimizing unnecessary effort. From a strategic perspective, this behavior, i can understand it. They may have limited time (or, limited inlaziness), multiple responsibilities, and competitive environments.

To be fair, this process is not entirely devoid of learning. Identifying patterns across previous examinations, recognizing recurring question types, and selecting efficient problem-solving strategies require analysis and metacognitive thinking. In many cases, students become remarkably skilled at understanding the structure of an assessment system. The issue is that this skill does not always translate into a deeper understanding of the subject itself.

This distinction between learning the subject and learning the exam is discussed in educational literature. Alfie Kohn argues that grading systems can redirect students' attention from learning toward performance, encouraging them to focus on obtaining favorable outcomes rather than developing understanding. He notes that students may begin asking whether material will appear on an exam rather than questioning its meaning or validity. Similarly, Dong and Liu's investigation of high-stakes testing demonstrates how assessments shape student motivation and learning behavior. Their findings suggest that examinations influence not only what students learn, but also how and why they learn it.

My own observations appear consistent with these ideas. Some students become highly effective at predicting examination patterns and reproducing familiar solution methods. They may achieve excellent scores while possessing only a fragmented understanding of the underlying concepts. Conversely, students who spend more time exploring ideas deeply sometimes perform less efficiently because their efforts are not always aligned with the assessment format.

This creates an interesting paradox. Education is intended to promote understanding, yet assessment systems can unintentionally reward optimization strategies. As a result, students may invest considerable effort not in mastering knowledge, but in mastering the mechanism used to measure knowledge.

I do not believe examinations should be abandoned. Assessments serve important purposes, and the ability to analyze patterns and develop effective strategies is itself a valuable skill. However, there is a difference between using strategy to demonstrate understanding and using strategy to substitute for understanding. When the latter becomes dominant, grades may no longer reflect what students know as much as how well they have learned the rules of the game.

Perhaps the most important question for educators and students alike is not whether examinations work, but what exactly they are encouraging students to learn. If students consistently devote more effort to predicting questions than understanding concepts, then the assessment system may be measuring something different from what it originally intended.

As a student, I do not claim to have an answer. I only find it difficult to ignore the possibility that many of us spend years becoming experts at passing examinations, while becoming less expert at learning itself.
